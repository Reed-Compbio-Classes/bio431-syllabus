# Bio431: Computational Image Analysis of Cells and Tissues

Biological image analysis is undergoing a computational revolution driven by advances in microscopy, machine learning, and the availability of large-scale datasets. This course will explore current computational methods for quantifying and analyzing microscopy images and movies of cells and tissues, illustrating how these approaches lead to biological discoveries. Students will use open-source tools to analyze publicly available and in-house microscopy datasets. Hands-on image analysis will be coupled with readings to build familiarity with the field through a combination of review articles, methodological papers, and primary literature. Topics may include automated cell segmentation and tracking, spatial organization of tissues, and cell migration during development and disease.

## Who this class is for

This class is for students who want to better understand how biological images are processed and analyzed. As part of this class, students will learn about computational concepts that have greatly influenced image analysis. Bio431 is a 0.5-unit upper-level biology course, and it is intended for students with junior or senior standing who have taken multiple biology courses. Students with an interest in image analysis from outside the sciences are also welcome to register after checking with me.

## Course goals

As a class, our goal is to develop an understanding of current computational image analysis methods and how they are used to answer biological questions. 

At the end of the semester, you will be able to:
1. Evaluate the strengths, limitations, and appropriate applications of contemporary computational image analysis methods in cellular biology.
2. Critically read and discuss scientific papers in computational image analysis.
3. Apply image analysis tools to biological datasets, interpret the resulting outputs, and justify methodological decisions.
4. Participate in a discussion around a specific scientific finding or set of findings derived from image analysis experiments.
5. Communicate technical concepts, methods, and results effectively in written and oral forms.
